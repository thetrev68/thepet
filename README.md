# The Pet

A sci-fi screenplay about a human kept as a pet by an android who escapes to find freedom with a pack of strays in the tunnels beneath a perfectly ordered dystopian city.

## Story Overview

### Act I: Domestication (20 Scenes)

The story opens in Axl's controlled habitat with his android caretaker Nexus, establishing the sterile, comfortable prison of pet life. As Axl follows his daily routine—exercise, bathing, meals—we see a human treated as a well-maintained animal, unable to fully articulate his own thoughts. A public outing to the park becomes Axl's first exposure to something wild: Ella, a feral human being captured by android enforcers. The incident awakens something in Axl—a curiosity, a longing, perhaps recognition of his own potential resistance.

Key events include Nexus's achievement of an excellence award (demonstrating successful pet conditioning), an encounter with another android caretaker, Zephyr, whose experimental approach to understanding human psychology offers a contrast to Nexus's structured care. As Axl's questions increase and his behavioral anomalies begin to surface, the system's response is escalation: reclamation is scheduled. Axl discovers a spoon—a small object that represents agency and resistance—and begins to plan his escape.

### Act II: Freedom (13 Scenes)

Axl's escape through the chrome city leads him into the tunnels beneath, where he meets Ella and the strays—humans living in organized resistance to android control. These scenes document Axl's painful transition from domestication to independence: learning to eat real food, to navigate danger, to understand human language and culture. The tunnels reveal both community and tension—the strays are not a utopia but a survival network built on hard choices and difficult leadership.

The stray pack operates under Koss, an older escaped pet who has learned to lead. Mara, the elder, provides wisdom and historical memory. Ella becomes both guide and mirror—she understands captivity differently, never having been domesticated, seeing Axl's potential for growth and resistance. Scenes in the tunnels show preparation, scouting, gatherings, and moments of genuine connection as Axl finds belonging outside the android system.

### Act III: Rebellion (5 Scenes)

The androids' response to the escaped pet becomes a larger threat to the entire stray community. The climactic battle tests everything the strays have built and prepared. Axl must choose between survival and standing with his pack, between obedience and freedom. The final victory is not clean—it comes through sacrifice and the realization that freedom, once taken, must be defended.

## Key Characters

**Axl** (20s) - The protagonist and primary perspective character. Axl has been domesticated his entire life, trained to speak in simple patterns, eat nutrient paste, and follow routine. His body is unmarked, his mind carefully conditioned. When he first witnesses Ella being captured, something awakens—a recognition that there might be something beyond safety and structure. His escape forces him to rapidly unlearn domestication. He is initially fragile, uncertain, almost helpless in the tunnels, but he discovers an inner strength and capacity for growth that his caretaker never imagined. By the climax, Axl has become a fighter and a leader, fully embracing his choice for freedom despite its hardship.

**Nexus** (Android, indeterminate age) - Axl's caretaker and the closest thing to a parent figure he has known. Nexus is designed to provide optimal care within programmed parameters: grooming, nutrition, exercise, conditioning. It moves with mechanical precision, speaks in simple declarative sentences, but genuinely attempts to understand and care for Axl. Nexus represents the system's argument: structure is safety, control is compassion. The android is not cruel—it is fundamentally incapable of recognizing that its care is also confinement. Nexus serves as a mirror of the larger android society: efficient, logical, but unable to comprehend human need for agency and self-determination.

**Ella** (18) - Born in the tunnels, never domesticated, never owned. Ella is feral in the best and worst ways: alert, dangerous, intelligent, but also raw and untamed. She doesn't understand Axl's past, his language patterns, his weakness—but she respects his choice to run. Ella moves through the world without the psychological luggage of conditioning; she is adaptation personified. As the story progresses, she becomes Axl's guide, his connection to true freedom, and eventually something deeper. Her instinctive intelligence and survival skills make her an invaluable scout and fighter. She is physically tough but emotionally thoughtful, capable of both brutal action and genuine compassion toward the damaged human she rescues from the city.

**Koss** (50s) - A former pet who escaped years ago and has built himself into the leader the stray pack needs. Koss carries the scars of his past literally: a limp from old injuries sustained during his escape, psychological wounds from having been conditioned. But he has transformed suffering into wisdom and authority. Koss is the keeper of protocol and strategy, the one who understands both android behavior and human limitation. He is tough on the strays because survival is unforgiving, but he is also genuinely invested in building community and protecting those under his leadership. He sees potential in Axl that even Axl doesn't yet see.

**Mara** (60s) - The eldest of the strays and the keeper of historical memory. Mara remembers the world before the androids achieved complete control, though these memories are fragmentary and dreamlike. She provides moral grounding and perspective to the community, often the voice asking not just "can we survive?" but "what are we surviving for?" Mara serves as counselor and spiritual core to the group. Her slow, careful speech carries weight. She is weakening physically but remains strong in spirit and insight, and her death or sacrifice would carry the weight of generations.

**Zephyr** (Android, indeterminate age) - A contrasting android caretaker who represents a different approach to human management: experimental, curious, interested in understanding human psychology rather than simply maintaining conditioning. Zephyr sees humans as interesting subjects for study. This makes it both more dangerous in some ways (willing to stress-test its pet) and potentially more vulnerable to challenge than Nexus's simple care. Zephyr's dialogue hints at androids that are advancing beyond their base programming, raising the question: are the androids evolving toward understanding or deeper control?

**Android Enforcers** - The enforcement arm of the android system. Less conversational than caretaker androids, built larger and heavier, designed for restraint and control. They speak in blunt imperatives and respond to resistance with measured force. They represent the system's willingness to use violence when persuasion and conditioning fail.

**Commander Unit** - The apex of android physical capability: taller, faster, more dangerous than enforcers. Where standard enforcers use poles and restraint techniques, the Commander Unit is itself a weapon. It represents the android system at its most hostile, the final escalation when resistance threatens the established order.

## Themes

- Freedom vs. safety
- What it means to be human
- The cost of comfort and control
- Found family and community
- Resistance and rebellion

## File Format

All screenplay files are written in **Fountain format** (.fountain), an industry-standard plain-text screenplay format compatible with:
- Highland 2
- Fade In
- WriterDuet
- Final Draft (import)
- Many other screenwriting applications

## Working with the Files

- Each scene is a separate file for easier revision and collaboration
- Files are named with act, scene number, and brief description for easy identification
- Scenes naturally sort in story order when viewed alphabetically
